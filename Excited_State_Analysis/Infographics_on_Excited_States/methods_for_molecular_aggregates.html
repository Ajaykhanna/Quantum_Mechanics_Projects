üß© Molecular Modeling Approaches

Three main strategies are used to simulate aggregates:

Cluster Models

Small groups (e.g., dimers) relevant to the process.

‚úî Pros: Computationally feasible, mechanistic insight
‚ùå Cons: Misses long-range and periodic effects

Exciton Models

Excitation treated as delocalized over chromophores.

‚úî Pros: Efficient for large systems, intuitive
‚ùå Cons: Requires vibronic/CT corrections

Full System Models

All atoms/molecules included explicitly.

‚úî Pros: Most accurate
‚ùå Cons: Extremely expensive; often intractable

Embedding techniques (e.g., Ewald, continuum) improve cluster model accuracy.

FOB-SH captures transport from small to large polaron diffusion, going beyond traditional perturbative models.

üî¥ Conical Intersections (CIs)

CIs are degeneracies between electronic states that enable ultrafast nonradiative decay.

Intra-CI

Localized on one molecule (e.g., bond twisting, proton transfer)

Leads to internal conversion (IC), emission quenching

Inter-CI

Between molecules in the aggregate

Enables ultrafast relaxation (e.g., <40 fs in A-D-A oligomers)

Observed in 2D spectroscopy and confirmed via nonadiabatic dynamics. A global PES approach is needed to model them accurately.

‚ú® Aggregation-Induced Emission (AIE)

AIE is enhanced luminescence upon aggregation, opposite to ACQ (aggregation-caused quenching).

Caused by suppression of nonradiative pathways via:

  • RIM: Restriction of Intramolecular Motion
  • RACI: Restricted Access to Conical Intersections

TVCF and global PES models are used to simulate AIE. Common in propeller-shaped molecules like tetraphenylethylene (TPE).

ü§ñ Machine Learning in Excited-State Modeling

ML is revolutionizing the field by accelerating simulations and enabling discovery:

  • Accelerates energy, gradient, and coupling calculations
  • Enables high-throughput screening of TADF, SF candidates
  • Predicts power conversion efficiency in OSCs
  • Assists in active space selection (e.g., auto-CAS)

Used in conjunction with exciton models, surface hopping, and transport theories for integrated workflows.

‚úÖ Conclusion & Recommendations

Key Takeaways

  1. No single method fits all ‚Äî choose based on system size, process, and accuracy.
  2. Exciton models are powerful but must include vibronic and CT effects.
  3. Conical intersections (intra- and intermolecular) control nonradiative decay.
  4. Multiconfigurational methods (CASPT2) are essential for SF and TTA.
  5. New dynamics methods (FOB-SH, ML) enable large-scale simulations beyond perturbative limits.
  6. Integration of theory and experiment drives innovation in OLEDs, OSCs, and phosphors.

For Students & Researchers

  • Start with TDDFT + dimer models for screening.
  • Use exciton models for optical properties and energy transfer.
  • Apply CDFT for CT states; CASPT2 for SF/TTA.
  • Leverage ML tools and libraries like fromage (Rivera et al.) and SHARC.
  • Validate simulations with experimental spectra and device data.
