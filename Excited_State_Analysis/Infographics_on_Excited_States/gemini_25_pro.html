Decoding Excited States:
TDDFT vs. TDA vs. sTDA

A visual guide to understanding the three key computational methods for studying how molecules interact with light.

The Core Mission

All three methods aim to solve the same problem: calculating the energy a molecule needs to absorb to jump from its stable ground state to an electronically excited state. This is fundamental to understanding color, photochemistry, and photovoltaics.

The Methods Compared

TDDFT

The "Gold Standard" Parent Method

  • Most complete and theoretically sound of the three.
  • Generally the most accurate for simple valence excitations.
  • Computationally expensive.
  • Can fail catastrophically (triplet instability).
  • Poor for charge-transfer states.

TDA

The Robust & Reliable Workhorse

  • Fixes the triplet instability problem completely.

  • Systematically overestimates excitation energies.
  • Theoretically less rigorous than full TDDFT.

The Swing Analogy

TDDFT

You push the swing (A Matrix) and also account for the subtle feedback from the swing's return motion helping your next push (B Matrix). It's a complete picture.

TDA

You only calculate the energy of your push (A Matrix) and completely ignore any feedback from the swing's return. It's simpler, more direct, but misses some of the physics.

Which Method Should I Choose?

  • Choose TDDFT if... You need the most accurate energy possible for a well-behaved valence excitation in a small-to-medium sized molecule, and you are not concerned about triplet states.
  • Choose TDA if... You are performing routine calculations, worried about triplet instabilities, or studying a system where TDDFT is known to perform poorly (like charge-transfer). This is the default "safe" choice for most applications.
  • Choose sTDA if... Your molecule is massive (a protein, a polymer, a large aggregate) and you just need a fast, qualitative idea of what its UV-Vis spectrum looks like. Speed is your absolute priority.
